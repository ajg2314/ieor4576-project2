# Geopolitical Analysis Framework for Sector Analysts

## Why Geopolitics Belongs in Every Sector Report

Financial markets price future cash flows. Geopolitical events affect future cash flows through:
1. **Supply chain disruption** — manufacturing concentration in geopolitically sensitive regions
2. **Market access restrictions** — export controls, sanctions, tariffs changing addressable markets
3. **Government subsidies / industrial policy** — altering competitive economics (CHIPS Act, IRA, EU Green Deal)
4. **Regulatory divergence** — EU vs. US vs. China rules creating compliance costs and market fragmentation
5. **Currency and macro effects** — strong dollar impacts multinationals' reported revenues

A sector report that ignores geopolitics is incomplete. The analyst's job is to assess probability-weighted impact, not just acknowledge risk exists.

## Key Frameworks by Sector

### Technology / Semiconductors
- US-China tech decoupling: assess revenue exposure to China per company (report as % of revenue)
- Export control risk: which products are at risk of new controls? (AI chips, quantum, biotech)
- Geographic manufacturing concentration (fab, assembly, testing)
- Government subsidy beneficiaries vs. those excluded (CHIPS Act guardrails)
- Talent and immigration policy: H-1B visa quotas affect tech hiring in US

### Energy
- OPEC+ production decisions and their revenue impact on oil majors
- Energy transition policy: IRA ($369B clean energy subsidies), EU Green Deal, carbon pricing
- Sanctions (Russia oil price cap, Iran, Venezuela): affects supply and pricing
- LNG geopolitics: Europe dependency post-Ukraine war; Asia-Pacific LNG demand
- Stranded asset risk: fossil fuel assets repriced by climate policy timelines

### Financials / Banking
- Interest rate policy: central bank decisions are the primary driver of net interest margin
- Basel III/IV capital requirements: affects ROE targets and dividend capacity
- Sanctions compliance: exposure to sanctioned entities/jurisdictions
- CBDCs (Central Bank Digital Currencies): potential disintermediation risk
- Crypto regulation: affects fintech valuations and banking sector disruption thesis

### Healthcare / Pharma
- Drug pricing policy: US IRA drug price negotiation provisions affect pharma margins
- FDA approval timelines and political pressure (accelerated approval reforms)
- China market access: China's VBP (Volume-Based Procurement) program compresses drug prices
- mRNA and biotech regulation: post-COVID regulatory framework evolution
- Global health emergencies as demand accelerators (COVID vaccine precedent)

### Consumer / Retail
- Tariffs: US-China tariffs affect supply chains for electronics, apparel, toys
- Consumer confidence and employment as primary demand drivers
- Currency risk: multinationals reporting in USD but earning in local currency
- ESG supply chain requirements: EU forced labor regulation, deforestation rules

### Automotive
- EV transition timelines: IRA EV tax credit rules, EU 2035 ICE ban
- China EV competition: BYD, NIO, CATL disrupting Western OEMs in key markets
- Battery supply chain: lithium, cobalt, nickel sourcing — geographic concentration in Congo, Chile, Australia
- Autonomous driving regulation: varies by jurisdiction, no unified framework

## How to Write the Geopolitical Section

**Structure it as: Event → Mechanism → Quantified Impact → Company-level Exposure**

Bad: "Trade tensions between the US and China pose risks to semiconductor companies."

Good: "US BIS export controls enacted in October 2024 restrict AI chip exports above 1,800 TFLOPS to all non-allied nations. NVIDIA derived 13% of FY2025 revenue ($17B) from China — this represents the maximum immediate revenue at risk. However, domestic Chinese demand has partially shifted to Huawei Ascend 910C chips, suggesting China customers are adapting rather than losing AI capability. The net impact is a permanent TAM reduction for NVIDIA of ~8-10% unless policy reverses."

**Always answer these questions:**
1. Which specific policy/event is relevant? (Name it, date it)
2. Which companies are most/least exposed? (Revenue %, geographic breakdown)
3. What is the mechanism of impact? (Revenue loss, cost increase, market exit, subsidy benefit)
4. What is the market already pricing vs. what is the residual risk? (Has the stock moved on this news?)
5. Are there second-order effects? (Competitors benefiting, supply chain restructuring, accelerated domestic alternatives)

## Global Events That Move Sectors

**Trade wars and tariffs**: US-China tariffs 2018-2019 and 2025 iterations affect any company with cross-border supply chains or markets. Assess by: (1) China revenue exposure, (2) China manufacturing exposure, (3) competitor geography.

**Wars and armed conflict**: Ukraine war (2022) caused energy price shock, fertilizer shortage, grain supply disruption. Israel-Gaza conflict affects Middle East operations. Direct effects on company operations in conflict zones; indirect effects through energy/commodity prices.

**Elections and policy change**: US presidential election cycles affect: tech regulation, energy policy (IRA at risk of rollback), defense spending, immigration (H-1B), drug pricing. EU elections affect: AI Act implementation, Green Deal speed, competition law enforcement.

**Pandemics and health emergencies**: COVID-19 created demand surges (semiconductors, cloud, pharma), supply chain collapses (auto, retail), and demand crashes (travel, hospitality). Demand that moved forward by 2 years led to inventory corrections in 2022-2023.

**Interest rate cycles**: Federal Reserve and ECB rate decisions are not "macro noise" — they directly affect: SaaS valuations (duration-sensitive), bank margins, housing-related sectors, consumer credit quality. Always note the current rate environment and direction.

**Central bank policy divergence**: When the Fed raises while ECB holds (or vice versa), currency moves follow. Companies with significant non-USD revenue see FX headwinds or tailwinds that can move reported revenue ±3-7%.

## Middle East: The Three Fault Lines That Move Markets

The Middle East contains three distinct geopolitical fault lines that analysts must assess independently. They interact but have different sector transmission mechanisms.

### Fault Line 1: The Iran Escalation Axis

**The Iran nuclear and proxy threat** is the dominant Middle East geopolitical variable for energy, defense, and shipping-exposed companies.

**Background**: Iran's nuclear enrichment has advanced to 60% purity (weapons-grade threshold is ~90%), giving it theoretical breakout capability within weeks as of 2024-2025. The US-Iran JCPOA nuclear deal remains effectively dead. US sanctions on Iranian oil exports have been inconsistently enforced; Iran has been exporting 1.5-2 million bbl/day to China via gray market.

**Iran's proxy network** ("Axis of Resistance") spans:
- **Hezbollah** (Lebanon): largest non-state missile arsenal in the world (~150,000 rockets); fought Israel along the northern front in 2024; ceasefire brokered Nov 2024 remains fragile
- **Hamas** (Gaza): October 7, 2023 attack triggered the Israel-Gaza war; as of 2025 Hamas military capacity is severely degraded but political organization persists
- **Houthis** (Yemen): Iranian-armed militia controlling western Yemen; launched 100+ attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea/Gulf of Aden from Dec 2023 through 2025
- **Iranian-backed militias** (Iraq, Syria): attacked US bases repeatedly in 2023-2024; US struck IRGC positions in response

**Iran-Israel direct confrontation (2024 escalation)**:
- April 13-14, 2024: Iran launched ~300 drones and missiles directly at Israel — first direct Iranian attack on Israeli territory. Israel/US/Jordan/Saudi Arabia intercepted ~99%. Markets spiked then recovered within 48 hours.
- October 1, 2024: Iran launched ~180 ballistic missiles at Israel. More sophisticated attack; Israel suffered minimal damage but promised severe retaliation.
- October 26, 2024: Israel struck Iranian air defense and military infrastructure sites in Isfahan and other locations — demonstrated ability to penetrate Iranian air defense undetected.
- 2025: Low-grade conflict continues via proxies; risk of another direct exchange remains elevated.

**Strait of Hormuz: The Critical Chokepoint**

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most important oil transit chokepoint. Key statistics:
- **~21 million barrels per day (bbl/day)** transit the strait — approximately 21% of global oil consumption and ~30% of global LNG trade
- Countries heavily dependent on Hormuz exports: Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, Iran itself
- Strait width: only 33km at narrowest point; easily disrupted by mines, naval blockade, or missile attacks
- **If Hormuz closed for 30 days**: Brent crude estimated to spike to $150-200/bbl; global LNG prices to 5-8x spot; airline fuel costs surge; industrial production curtailed

**Iran has threatened Hormuz closure** during multiple confrontations. Closure probability is considered low (Iran itself exports through Hormuz) but the tail risk justifies premium in energy stocks and defense sector pricing. Even partial disruption (piracy attacks, insurance risk premiums) elevates shipping costs significantly.

**Sector impact of Iran/Hormuz escalation**:
- **Energy** (XOM, CVX, COP, SHEL, BP): higher oil price benefits upstream producers; refining margins vary. Each $10/bbl increase in Brent = ~$1.2B annual operating income for a major like Exxon.
- **Defense** (LMT, RTX, NOC, GD, BA): increased US/allied procurement; Iron Dome, THAAD, Patriot missile system demand. RTX received $1.2B+ Patriot orders in 2024.
- **Airlines** (UAL, DAL, AAL): jet fuel is 20-25% of operating cost; $10/bbl increase = ~$500M annual cost increase for a major US carrier.
- **Shipping/Logistics** (ZIM, MAERSK, Hapag-Lloyd): Red Sea diversions around Cape of Good Hope add 10-14 days to Europe-Asia routes, consuming additional fuel at ~$50,000/day per vessel.
- **Insurance**: Lloyd's of London war risk premiums on vessels in Red Sea/Gulf of Aden rose 10-20x from early 2023 to 2024. Even post-ceasefire, elevated.

### Fault Line 2: The Red Sea Crisis and Houthi Disruption

Starting December 2023, Houthi forces (Yemen, Iranian-backed) began systematically attacking commercial vessels in the Red Sea and Bab el-Mandeb strait, claiming solidarity with Palestinians in Gaza.

**Scale of disruption**:
- Suez Canal traffic fell ~60% from peak levels by Q1 2024 as shipping lines rerouted
- Cape of Good Hope diversions add 10-14 days, ~$1M-1.5M in extra fuel per round trip
- Container shipping rates (Freightos Baltic Index) rose 200-400% from Dec 2023 trough to Q1 2024 peak
- Major shipping lines (Maersk, MSC, Hapag-Lloyd, COSCO) suspended Red Sea transit

**US and allies launched Operation Prosperity Guardian** (Dec 2023) to protect shipping; USS Eisenhower carrier group and allied destroyers have intercepted hundreds of Houthi missiles and drones. Despite this, Houthi attacks continued through 2024-2025.

**Corporate impacts**:
- **Retailers/consumer discretionary**: supply chain delays and cost increases; H&M, Ikea, Tesla (European factory halted briefly), Volvo reported disruptions
- **Energy companies**: LNG tanker routes affected; European LNG spot prices elevated
- **Defense**: US military costs of Operation Prosperity Guardian estimated at $1B+ through 2024

**Sector transmission**:
- Inflation re-acceleration risk: shipping costs feed into goods inflation with 3-6 month lag
- Just-in-time manufacturing disrupted: auto OEMs, electronics, apparel most exposed
- Companies with high Asia-Europe shipment volumes are most at risk: AAPL (iPhone components), NKE (manufacturing in Vietnam/Indonesia), retailers

### Fault Line 3: Saudi Arabia and UAE Strategic Positioning

Saudi Arabia and UAE are not adversaries — they are critical US energy and strategic partners, but with increasingly independent foreign policies.

**Saudi Arabia key dynamics**:
- Saudi Arabia controls the world's largest oil spare capacity (~2-3 million bbl/day of swing production)
- OPEC+ decision-making is Saudi-led; production cuts/increases set global oil price floor/ceiling
- **Vision 2030**: MBS's economic diversification program reduces Saudi economy's oil dependence long-term; creates investment opportunities in tourism, entertainment, tech
- **Saudi Aramco** (2222.SR, not US-listed): largest global oil producer; 10% IPO in 2019 valued it at $1.7T+. US investors have indirect exposure through Aramco supply contracts and Saudi sovereign wealth fund (PIF) investments in US stocks.
- Saudi Arabia is a key buyer of US defense equipment (F-15, Patriot, THAAD); US arms sales are a significant lever in the bilateral relationship
- **Iran-Saudi normalization (March 2023)**: China-brokered Iran-Saudi diplomatic restoration was a major geopolitical surprise. Saudi-Iran tensions reduced but underlying rivalry persists.
- **US-Saudi tensions**: US Biden administration's human rights criticism vs. Trump-era partnership; arms sales occasionally suspended; OPEC+ production cuts caused friction with Washington in 2022-2024

**UAE key dynamics**:
- UAE (especially Dubai/Abu Dhabi) positions itself as a neutral hub: Russia, Iran, Israel, and the US all maintain commercial relationships in the UAE
- **Houthi missile attacks on UAE** (Jan 2022): Houthis struck Abu Dhabi airport and oil infrastructure; created brief concern about UAE stability. UAE co-leads anti-Houthi coalition.
- **UAE-Israel Abraham Accords (2020)**: Normalization opened Israel-UAE commercial aviation, banking, and trade links
- UAE's sovereign wealth fund (ADIA, Mubadala) is a major global investor: significant stakes in US private equity, tech, and real estate

**Sector impacts of Saudi/UAE dynamics**:
- **Energy**: Saudi production decisions set Brent price floor; an OPEC+ production increase can crush oil company revenue within 3-6 months
- **Defense**: Arms sales to Saudi and UAE are a major revenue line for RTX, LMT, Boeing Defense; subject to US Congressional approval and diplomatic conditions
- **Real estate/finance**: UAE and Saudi sovereign wealth capital flows into global assets

## China-US Strategic Competition: The Defining Rivalry

The China-US relationship is the single most important bilateral geopolitical factor for global equity markets. It operates across five vectors simultaneously.

### Vector 1: Technology and Semiconductor Decoupling

**Export controls timeline**:
- Oct 2022: US BIS restricts advanced semiconductor exports to China; foreign direct product rule (FDPR) extended to US technology used by non-US companies (ASML, Samsung, TSMC effectively forced to comply)
- Oct 2023: BIS expands controls, closes loopholes from Oct 2022; A100/H100 restricted; AMD MI300 restricted
- Oct 2024: BIS introduces tiered country licensing framework; further restricts data center AI chip exports; Huawei Ascend 910C placed on Entity List
- 2025: Controls extended to advanced packaging, HBM memory, and semiconductor equipment (AMAT, Lam Research, KLA restricted for certain tools)
- Japan (ASML-equivalent DUV tools) and Netherlands (ASML EUV, immersion DUV) aligned with US controls under allied pressure by 2024

**China's domestic semiconductor response**:
- SMIC (Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corp): advancing to 7nm using multi-patterning on legacy DUV tools; breakthrough in 2023 demonstrated with Huawei Mate 60 Pro (7nm chip). SMIC remains 2-3 generations behind TSMC.
- China government investment: $40B+ in dedicated semiconductor funds (Big Fund II and III) supporting SMIC, Hua Hong, YMTC (memory), CXMT (DRAM)
- Huawei Ascend 910B/910C: competitive with NVIDIA A100-class for AI training; Huawei sold ~400,000 units in 2024 per analyst estimates. Performance gap vs. H100/B200 remains meaningful (roughly 50-70% of performance at higher power)
- China's best chips: ~7nm logic (2 generations behind), ~128-layer NAND (competitive), ~21nm DRAM (behind SK Hynix HBM3E)

**Company-level impacts**:
- NVIDIA: China revenue historically 20-25% of total; now ~10-15% due to controls. A100/H100/B200 banned; downgraded H20 chip created for China market subject to further restrictions. NVDA lost ~$10B+ in annual China revenue opportunity.
- ASML: ~30% China revenue in 2023; DUV immersion machines banned Jan 2024; pure DUV allowed for mature nodes. China revenue at risk of further decline to ~15-20% of total.
- AMAT (Applied Materials): ~27% China revenue; advanced tool restrictions; legacy tools still sold legally.
- Lam Research: ~30% China revenue; similar restriction profile to AMAT.
- Intel: China revenue ~27% of 2023 total; Xeon server chips restricted for some applications; foundry business restrictions.
- Qualcomm: ~62% China revenue (handset chips); not yet directly controlled but subject to escalation risk; Chinese OEMs (Xiaomi, OPPO, vivo) are primary customers.

### Vector 2: Tariffs and Trade War (2025 Escalation)

**2025 tariff escalation**:
The Trump administration (returning to office Jan 2025) implemented sweeping new tariff measures:
- **60%+ tariffs on all Chinese goods** proposed/enacted by the administration in 2025 rounds
- China retaliated with tariffs on US agricultural goods, energy, and autos
- Some exemptions negotiated for specific categories (electronics components where alternatives don't exist)
- Result: significant cost pressure on US companies manufacturing in China (Apple, Nike, consumer electronics OEMs)

**Supply chain repositioning** (multi-year trend):
- "China+1" strategy: US companies diversifying manufacturing to Vietnam, Mexico, India, Thailand
- Apple: shifting iPhone manufacturing to India (Foxconn Tata); AirPods to Vietnam; Mac to Malaysia
- Nike: >50% manufacturing in Vietnam, Indonesia
- Mexico near-shoring: US-Mexico trade now exceeds US-China trade for first time in decades

### Vector 3: Rare Earths and Critical Minerals

China controls an estimated 60-85% of global rare earth processing capacity, even when ore comes from elsewhere.

**Key materials and Chinese dominance**:
- Rare earth elements (REEs): China processes ~85% globally; used in EV motors, wind turbine generators, MRI machines, precision guided weapons
- Gallium and Germanium: China controls ~80% of global gallium production, ~60% of germanium; both are key semiconductor dopants. China imposed export restrictions on both in Aug 2023.
- Graphite: China produces ~67% of global natural graphite; critical for lithium-ion battery anodes. Export licensing restrictions imposed Oct 2023.
- Antimony: China controls ~48% of global supply; used in ammunition and semiconductors; export restrictions tightened 2024.
- Cobalt: DR Congo produces ~70% but Chinese companies (CMOC, Zhejiang Huayou) control significant refining capacity

**Sector impact**:
- **Defense**: DOD has flagged rare earth supply chain vulnerability repeatedly; US is building domestic rare earth processing (MP Materials, Energy Fuels) but substitution takes 5-10 years
- **EV**: battery supply chains exposed to Chinese graphite, cobalt, lithium processing; IRA domestic content rules forcing supply chain shifts
- **Semiconductors**: gallium arsenide and gallium nitride used in RF chips (QCOM, Skyworks); export restrictions increase cost uncertainty

### Vector 4: Taiwan Strait

Taiwan is the world's leading producer of advanced semiconductors (TSMC produces ~92% of chips below 10nm globally). Military conflict or a blockade scenario would be catastrophic for the global technology industry.

**Risk scenarios**:
- **Blockade (most likely escalation path)**: China surrounds Taiwan with naval and air forces, preventing shipping. TSMC exports halted. Global chip shortage worse than 2021-2022 within 6-12 months.
- **Missile/air strikes on TSMC**: China might target TSMC fabs to prevent Taiwan from becoming a "chip fortress." TSMC has noted fabs would be rendered inoperable — scorched earth deterrence.
- **Full invasion**: Extremely high cost for China in casualties and global economic isolation; considered low probability near-term but risk has risen with PLA buildup.

**What has the market priced?**
Most equity analysts apply a minimal "Taiwan war premium" to TSMC (TSM) shares. TSM trades at a discount to US-listed semiconductor peers — analysts call this the "geopolitical discount." The market appears to be pricing ~5-10% probability of severe disruption over a 10-year horizon. If probability estimates rise materially, TSM could de-rate 20-30%.

**TSMC mitigation**:
- TSMC Arizona (Phoenix): 2nm fab under construction with CHIPS Act support; producing 3nm chips planned for 2025. Capacity: ~10% of TSMC global by 2027.
- TSMC Japan (Kumamoto): 6/12nm fab; opened 2024.
- TSMC Germany (Dresden): mature node fab in planning.
- These facilities provide some geographic diversification but do NOT replicate TSMC's leading-edge capacity in Taiwan — that knowledge/talent concentration is irreplaceable near-term.

### Vector 5: Financial System Competition

- **Dollar weaponization**: US use of SWIFT exclusions and dollar-clearing restrictions as sanctions tools (Russia 2022) has prompted China, Russia, Saudi Arabia, and others to accelerate de-dollarization efforts
- **BRICS currency discussions**: "mBridge" cross-border CBDC platform developed by China/UAE/Saudi/Thailand; not yet widely adopted but represents a long-term dollar alternative
- **Chinese sovereign debt in US Treasury market**: China reduced UST holdings from $1.3T (2013 peak) to ~$750B (2024); not yet a market-moving exodus but a strategic signaling tool
- **Ant Group, TikTok, Shein**: US regulatory pressure on Chinese consumer/fintech firms reflects technology-nationalism; creates precedent for broader Chinese corporate access restrictions

## Russia-Ukraine War: The European Energy and Defense Restructuring

### Energy Impact

Russia's invasion of Ukraine (Feb 24, 2022) triggered the largest restructuring of European energy infrastructure since the 1970s oil shock.

**Pre-war dependence**:
- Europe imported ~40% of natural gas from Russia (Germany ~55%, Italy ~46%, Austria ~80%)
- Nord Stream 1 and 2 pipelines were primary delivery mechanism (combined capacity ~110 bcm/year)
- Nord Stream 2 never opened; Nord Stream 1 and 2 were sabotaged in September 2022 (unsolved)

**Post-war energy restructuring**:
- **Europe's LNG imports surged**: EU imported ~170 bcm LNG in 2023 vs. ~100 bcm in 2021; US became Europe's largest LNG supplier
- **US LNG beneficiaries**: Cheniere (LNG), Venture Global LNG, New Fortress Energy; European LNG terminals built in record time (Germany built 4 FSRUs in 6 months in 2022)
- **Remaining Russian gas flows**: Russia still supplies gas via TurkStream pipeline and via Ukraine transit corridor (Ukrainian transit deal expired Jan 2025; Hungary and Slovakia still receiving Russian gas via alternative routes)
- **EU energy prices**: TTF natural gas hit €300/MWh peak (2022); normalized to €30-50/MWh by 2024 but structural premium vs. pre-war persists
- **Energy independence imperative**: EU accelerated renewable buildout; Germany's Energiewende targets 80% renewable electricity by 2030; offshore wind, solar, hydrogen investments accelerated

**Oil sanctions**:
- G7 oil price cap ($60/bbl on Russian crude) implemented Dec 2022
- Russia sells oil at discounts to India, China, Turkey — effectively circumventing cap
- Urals crude typically at $10-15/bbl discount to Brent since war; Russia lost ~$20-30B/year in oil revenue vs. pre-war estimates
- Western oil majors (Shell, BP, Equinor) wrote down Russia asset exits: BP wrote off ~$24B, Shell ~$5B, Exxon ~$4B

### Defense Spending Surge

The war demonstrated that large-scale conventional warfare in Europe is possible, shattering post-Cold War assumptions. NATO members have dramatically increased defense budgets.

**NATO 2% GDP target compliance**:
- Pre-war (2021): Only 6-8 NATO members met the 2% target
- Post-war (2025): ~20+ NATO members are at or above 2% target
- Poland: 4% of GDP on defense — highest in NATO; largest European defense procurement in a generation (F-35, HIMARS, K2 tanks from South Korea)
- Germany: ended "Zeitenwende" (historic shift); ended balanced budget rule to fund €100B special defense fund; buying F-35s, Patriot systems, submarines
- European defense stocks: Rheinmetall (Germany) up 700%+ from pre-war; BAE Systems up 200%+; Thales, Leonardo, Saab all significantly re-rated

**US defense beneficiaries**:
- **Lockheed Martin (LMT)**: F-35 (Poland, Germany, others), HIMARS (Ukraine/NATO), Patriot (Ukraine/NATO); backlog grew ~$30B+ since war began
- **RTX (formerly Raytheon)**: Stinger MANPADS (depleted US stockpiles must be replenished); Patriot PAC-3 missiles (hundreds fired in Ukraine); multi-year production ramp required
- **General Dynamics (GD)**: Abrams tank production; howitzer shells (155mm shortage created by Ukrainian consumption)
- **Northrop Grumman (NOC)**: ammunition, GBSD nuclear modernization, B-21 bomber
- Artillery shell consumption: Ukraine has consumed more 155mm shells per day than US/NATO could produce per month; triggered industrial base investments across the alliance

### Commodity and Supply Chain Impacts

**Ukraine agricultural disruption**:
- Ukraine is world's 5th largest wheat exporter, largest sunflower oil exporter, major corn exporter
- Odessa port blockade (2022) caused global grain price spike; Black Sea Grain Initiative allowed some exports before Russia suspended it in 2023
- Fertilizer: Belarus and Russia are top potash exporters; sanctions and war disrupted global fertilizer markets; food inflation in developing countries

**Critical minerals from Russia and Ukraine**:
- **Palladium**: Russia produces ~40% of global palladium (catalytic converters); prices spiked 2022, normalized by 2024 as auto demand fell and recycling increased
- **Nickel**: Russia produces ~20% of high-grade nickel (EV batteries, stainless steel); London Metal Exchange nickel short-squeeze March 2022 was historically unprecedented
- **Titanium**: Russia produces ~35% of aerospace-grade titanium; Boeing and Airbus had significant Russian titanium exposure (both have been substituting)
- **Uranium**: Kazakhstan (not Russia) is largest producer, but Russia's Rosatom controls enrichment for many countries; US reliance on Russian-enriched uranium being addressed by 2024 Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act

### Ceasefire/Peace Negotiation Scenarios

By 2025, pressure for negotiated settlement has increased:
- US (Trump administration) has pushed for negotiated end to war
- Possible scenarios: frozen conflict along current lines (Russia holds ~20% of Ukraine territory), formal ceasefire, or continued conflict
- **Investment implications of ceasefire**: European reconstruction opportunity (Ukraine GDP was ~$200B pre-war; reconstruction estimated at $500B+); materials, engineering, construction companies; potential easing of energy premium as risk perception falls; Russian asset write-backs for companies that preserved claims

## Analyst Checklist: Mapping Geopolitical Exposure

For any sector analysis, apply this checklist:

**Middle East / Strait of Hormuz**
- Does the company have significant energy cost exposure? (airlines, chemicals, plastics, shipping)
- Does the company produce or sell oil and gas? (direct price impact)
- Does the company rely on Red Sea shipping routes? (Asia-Europe supply chains)
- Is the company a defense contractor selling to Gulf states or supplying US military?
- Does the company have operations or revenue in Israel, UAE, Saudi Arabia, or adjacent markets?

**China-US**
- What % of revenue comes from China? (disclose in annual reports — look for geographic segment)
- What % of manufacturing or sourcing is in China? (tariff cost pass-through risk)
- Does the company use chips, rare earths, or materials with Chinese supply chain concentration?
- Is the company or its products subject to current or potential US export controls?
- Does the company compete directly with Chinese state-backed companies?

**Russia-Ukraine**
- Does the company have residual Russia exposure (direct operations, unsettled write-downs)?
- Does the company source palladium, nickel, titanium, or other Russian commodities?
- Is the company a defense contractor benefiting from NATO ramp-up?
- Does the company sell LNG, renewable energy equipment, or grid infrastructure to Europe?
- Does the company have grain, fertilizer, or agricultural exposure?
