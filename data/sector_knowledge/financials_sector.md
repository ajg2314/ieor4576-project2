# Financial Services Sector Knowledge Base

## Business Models

### Commercial Banks
JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citigroup. Core business: accept deposits (liability) and make loans (asset). Net Interest Income (NII) = interest earned on loans/securities - interest paid on deposits. Non-interest income: fees, trading revenue, wealth management, investment banking.

**Revenue drivers**: Interest rate environment (higher rates → wider NIM, more NII); loan growth (credit demand); credit quality (loan losses eat into revenue); fee income from services.

### Investment Banks / Broker-Dealers
Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley (plus the investment banking divisions of JPMorgan, BofA, Citi). Revenue from M&A advisory fees, equity/debt underwriting, trading (market making), prime brokerage, asset management.

Investment banking revenue is highly cyclical: bull markets and low rates drive M&A and IPO activity; bear markets suppress fee income. Trading revenue (FICC — fixed income, currencies, commodities) is more countercyclical.

### Insurance
Berkshire Hathaway (insurance subsidiary), Chubb, AIG, Aflac, Prudential. Revenue from premiums written; profit comes from:
1. **Underwriting profit**: Premiums received - claims paid - operating expenses
2. **Investment income**: Insurers invest float (premium collected before claims paid) in bonds and equities

**Combined ratio** (key metric): (Losses + Expenses) / Premiums Earned. Below 100% = underwriting profit. Above 100% = underwriting loss (must rely on investment income to be profitable).

### Asset Management
BlackRock, Vanguard, Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, Franklin Templeton. Revenue from management fees (% of AUM — assets under management). Fee compression is the structural challenge: rise of passive/index funds (charging 0.03–0.10%) competes with active managers (charging 0.50–1.0%+).

**AUM dynamics**: AUM grows through market appreciation and net flows (new client money - redemptions). A 20% market decline drops AUM (and fee revenue) by 20% without any change in client numbers.

### Financial Technology (Fintech)
Visa, Mastercard (payment networks), PayPal, Block (Square), Coinbase, SoFi. Payment networks earn fees on transaction volume (typically 0.1–0.3% of payment value). Revenue grows with consumer spending volumes, not credit risk.

## Critical Financial Metrics

### Net Interest Margin (NIM)
```
NIM = (Interest Income from Loans and Securities - Interest Paid on Deposits) / Average Earning Assets
```
- Typical range: 2.5–4.5% for US commercial banks
- Rises when rates increase (banks reprice loans faster than deposits)
- Compress when rates fall (deposit floors limit how low funding costs go)
- **Interest rate sensitivity**: A 100bps (1%) Fed rate increase typically expands bank NIM by 20–50bps, adding 10–20% to net interest income for rate-sensitive banks

### Loan Loss Provisions
Banks set aside provisions for expected credit losses before they occur (CECL — Current Expected Credit Loss standard, adopted 2020 for large banks).

```
Net Charge-Off Rate = (Loans charged off - Recoveries) / Average Loans
```
- Typical NCO rates in a benign credit environment: 0.2–0.5% for commercial banks
- In a recession: rise to 1–3%+ for consumer credit; higher for subprime

**Provision expense = income statement item**: High provisions reduce current period earnings but improve future loss resilience. Rising provisions signal credit deterioration.

### Basel III/IV Capital Ratios

Banks must hold minimum regulatory capital as a buffer against losses. Three key ratios:

| Ratio | Formula | Minimum Requirement |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| CET1 (Common Equity Tier 1) | Common equity - Goodwill / Risk-Weighted Assets | ≥ 4.5% (+ buffers, typically ~7–11%) |
| Tier 1 Capital Ratio | Tier 1 Capital / Risk-Weighted Assets | ≥ 6% |
| Total Capital Ratio | Total Capital / Risk-Weighted Assets | ≥ 8% |
| Leverage Ratio | Tier 1 / Total Exposure (not risk-weighted) | ≥ 3–5% |

**Basel III Endgame (US proposal, 2023)**: Proposed to increase risk-weighted assets by 20%+ for large banks, reducing their CET1 ratios by ~200bps and limiting buybacks. Banks lobbied heavily; final rules expected to be scaled back from initial proposal.

**Why capital ratios matter for investing**:
- High CET1 (>13%): Excess capital; potential buybacks/dividends = positive for shareholders
- CET1 falling toward minimums: Bank must retain earnings, limit buybacks; may need to issue equity
- Stress test results (Fed DFAST): Determines maximum shareholder returns banks can distribute

### Return on Equity (ROE) and Return on Assets (ROA)
```
ROE = Net Income / Average Shareholders' Equity  (target: >10-15% for large banks)
ROA = Net Income / Average Total Assets           (target: >1% for US banks)
```

ROE > cost of equity (typically 10–12% for banks) = value creation. JPMorgan's ROE of ~17% is exceptional; European banks at 5–8% trade below book value because ROE < cost of equity.

### Efficiency Ratio
```
Efficiency Ratio = Non-Interest Expenses / Revenue (NII + Non-Interest Income)
```
Lower = more efficient. Best-in-class US banks: 50–55%. Average: 60–65%. Above 70% = poor cost control.

### Tangible Book Value (TBV)
```
TBV Per Share = (Shareholders' Equity - Goodwill - Intangibles) / Shares Outstanding
```
Banks with P/TBV > 2x have strong ROE. P/TBV < 1x means market doubts the bank can earn its cost of capital (common for European banks post-2008).

## Interest Rate Sensitivity

The single most important macro variable for bank stocks is the Federal Reserve's policy rate.

**How rate hikes affect banks**:
1. **Short-term positive (NIM expansion)**: Variable-rate loans (HELOCs, commercial loans) reprice immediately. Fixed-rate deposit accounts reprice slowly (depositors don't immediately demand higher rates). This widens NIM.
2. **Medium-term risk (deposit beta)**: As rates stay high, depositors shift to money market funds and higher-yielding savings accounts ("deposit repricing"). This raises funding costs and compresses NIM.
3. **Long-term risk (credit quality)**: Higher rates increase debt service burden on borrowers. Commercial real estate (CRE) is most vulnerable — floating rate loans with falling asset values = credit losses.
4. **Bond portfolio mark-to-market**: Rising rates reduce the fair value of banks' securities portfolios. Silicon Valley Bank (March 2023) collapsed partly due to unrealized losses on held-to-maturity bonds becoming realized when they sold assets to meet deposit outflows.

**Deposit beta**: The % of rate increases passed through to deposit rates. A 50% deposit beta means if rates rise 100bps, deposits get 50bps higher rates. High deposit beta = NIM expansion limited; low deposit beta = NIM benefits accrue to the bank longer.

### Inverted Yield Curve (Short Rates > Long Rates)
Banks borrow short (deposits) and lend long (mortgages, term loans). An inverted yield curve (as seen in 2022–2023) compresses NIM because short-term funding costs exceed long-term loan yields. Duration risk: banks with long-duration bond portfolios suffer mark-to-market losses when rates rise.

## Credit Cycle Dynamics

The credit cycle is the most important medium-term driver of bank profitability.

### Early Cycle (Post-Recession Recovery)
- Credit conditions loosen; lending standards ease
- Loan growth accelerates; charge-offs fall from recessionary peaks
- Banks release loan loss reserves (reserve release = earnings tailwind)
- Stock performance: strong; ROE expands

### Mid-Cycle (Expansion)
- Credit growth healthy; defaults low; competition intensifies
- Lending standards gradually loosen; covenants erode ("covenant-lite" loans)
- NIM stable or compressing (rate competition for deposits)
- Leverage builds in the system (corporate debt/EBITDA ratios rise)

### Late Cycle (Pre-Recession)
- Credit spreads widen; risk appetite falls
- Banks tighten lending standards; loan growth slows
- Charge-offs begin rising from cyclical trough
- Provisions increase; ROE contracts

### Recession / Stress
- Charge-offs spike; provisions surge; losses consume capital
- Write-downs on commercial real estate, leveraged loans
- Weaker banks face regulatory intervention or failure
- Stock performance: severe underperformance; dividend cuts

**Key early warning indicators**:
- Rising delinquency rates (30+ and 90+ days past due)
- Increasing classified/criticized loan ratios
- Falling commercial real estate valuations (office sector 2023–2024)
- Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS) — Fed survey showing banks tightening standards

## Insurance-Specific Metrics

### Loss Ratio
```
Loss Ratio = (Losses Incurred + Loss Adjustment Expenses) / Net Premiums Earned
```
Measures claims experience. A loss ratio of 65% means $65 of every $100 in premium goes to paying claims.

### Expense Ratio
```
Expense Ratio = Underwriting Expenses / Net Premiums Written
```
Operating efficiency of the underwriting operation. Lower = better.

### Combined Ratio
```
Combined Ratio = Loss Ratio + Expense Ratio
```
Below 100% = underwriting profit. Above 100% = underwriting loss. Insurance companies with combined ratios of 95–98% are well-run; 80s = exceptional.

**Catastrophe events**: Hurricane, earthquake, wildfire events spike the loss ratio. Reinsurers (Munich Re, Swiss Re) absorb excess losses above retention levels. Climate change is increasing "secondary peril" frequency (wildfires, flooding).

### Investment Float
Berkshire Hathaway's insurance float ($160B+) is essentially a free or negative-cost funding source. Premiums received in advance of claims are invested. If underwriting is profitable (combined ratio < 100%), Berkshire is paid to hold the float.

## Asset Management Dynamics

### Fee Compression
Average active equity fund management fee: fell from ~1.0% in 2000 to ~0.5% by 2024. Passive/index fund fees: 0.03–0.10%. The shift from active to passive has been structural: since 2010, US equity passive funds have received net inflows while active funds have seen net outflows in aggregate.

**Winners**: Vanguard, BlackRock (iShares), State Street (SPDR) dominate passive. BlackRock's scale ($10T+ AUM) creates cost advantages impossible for smaller active managers to match.

**Adaptation**: Asset managers are moving into alternatives (private equity, private credit, hedge funds) where fees remain 1-2% plus carried interest (20% of profits). BlackRock acquired Global Infrastructure Partners ($3B deal, 2024) and Preqin to expand alternatives data. Apollo, KKR, Blackstone are pure alternative asset managers with higher fee structures.

### AUM Growth Decomposition
```
AUM growth = Market return × Beginning AUM + Net Flows
```
When markets fall 20%, AUM-based fee revenue falls 20% instantly. Net flows (new client money) take years to rebuild. This leverage to market performance makes asset manager stocks highly beta-correlated to equity market returns.

## Geopolitical Factors for Financial Sector

### US Banking Regulation
- **Dodd-Frank (2010)**: Enhanced capital requirements, Volcker Rule (limits proprietary trading), stress tests (DFAST)
- **Basel III Endgame (proposed 2023)**: Would require large banks to hold more capital against operational risk and market risk. Banks lobbied aggressively; final rule expected to be significantly scaled back
- **Regional bank stress (2023)**: SVB, Signature, First Republic failures due to concentration risk, rate sensitivity, and deposit runs. Led to proposed enhanced regulation for banks $100B–$250B

### Global Systemic Risk
Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs): JPMorgan, BofA, Citi, Goldman, Wells Fargo (US); HSBC, Barclays (UK); UBS, Credit Suisse (Switzerland — CS rescued by UBS, 2023); Deutsche Bank (Germany). G-SIBs face additional capital buffers of 1–3.5% on top of Basel minimums.

### Interest Rate Policy (Fed, ECB, BoJ)
- Fed rate path is the #1 macro driver of US bank stocks
- ECB rate normalization (2022–2023) ended negative rate era in Europe; boosted European bank profitability for the first time in a decade
- Bank of Japan YCC (yield curve control) abandonment (2024) allows Japanese government bond yields to rise; impacts Japanese bank profitability and global bond markets

## EDA Playbook for Financial Services

1. **Revenue decomposition**: Separate NII, non-interest income (fees, trading), and provisions. Understand which driver is responsible for any revenue change.
2. **NIM trend**: A rising NIM in a rate-hike cycle is expected. If NIM is falling despite rising rates, check deposit beta and funding cost dynamics.
3. **Capital ratio trajectory**: CET1 rising = capital building (may precede buybacks); CET1 falling = earnings weakness or RWA growth
4. **Efficiency ratio trend**: Falling = productivity gains (tech investment paying off); rising = revenue headwinds outpacing cost cuts
5. **Loan growth**: Above-GDP loan growth = market share gain or loosening standards (investigate which); below-GDP = credit contraction or shrinkage
6. **Credit quality**: Delinquency rates and charge-offs are lagging indicators. Provision expense is a leading indicator (management forward-looking expectation).
7. **For insurance**: Always start with combined ratio trend. Rising combined ratio = either catastrophe losses or pricing inadequacy.
8. **For asset managers**: Separate AUM growth into market returns vs. net flows. Net outflows in a rising market = structural problem (active → passive shift).
9. **NEVER use average revenue for banks**: Bank revenue is highly cyclical and rate-sensitive. Use most recent quarter and trailing-12-month figures to understand current profitability, not multi-year averages.
10. **Interpret large one-time charges**: Legal settlements (JPMorgan $13B MBS settlement 2013), goodwill write-downs, and restructuring charges are non-recurring. Adjust earnings to get normalized ROE.
