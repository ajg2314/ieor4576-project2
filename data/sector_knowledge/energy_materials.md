# Energy & Materials Sector Knowledge Base

## Industry Structure

### Upstream (Exploration & Production — E&P)
Companies like ExxonMobil, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, Pioneer Natural Resources, Devon Energy. Revenue comes from selling crude oil and natural gas at market prices. This is the most commodity-sensitive segment — revenue swings directly with oil/gas prices. When Brent crude moves from $60/bbl to $80/bbl, upstream revenue increases proportionally if volumes are constant.

Key economics: Production cost (or "lifting cost") is the cash cost to extract a barrel of oil or mcf of gas. US shale breakeven: $40–55/bbl (varies by basin). Middle East NOCs: $5–15/bbl. Producers with lifting cost well below current price generate high free cash flow.

**How upstream companies make money**: Price - Lifting Cost - DD&A (depreciation of wells) - G&A = Operating Income. Free cash flow is the key metric — it determines dividend sustainability and buyback capacity.

### Midstream (Pipelines, Storage, LNG Terminals)
Companies like Kinder Morgan, Williams Companies, Enterprise Products Partners, Cheniere Energy. Revenue comes from fees for transporting, processing, and storing hydrocarbons. Less commodity-sensitive than upstream because fee-based contracts provide revenue visibility regardless of commodity prices.

Midstream is essentially infrastructure — long-term take-or-pay contracts (the customer pays whether they ship product or not) underpin stable cash flows. EBITDA margins are typically 35–50%.

**LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas) export economics**: US has the world's lowest-cost natural gas (Henry Hub typically $2–4/MMBtu). LNG terminals cool gas to -162°C, load onto tankers, ship to Asia/Europe where JKM (Japan-Korea Marker) prices trade at $8–20/MMBtu. Cheniere captures the spread between Henry Hub + liquefaction cost (~$3/MMBtu) and international prices. Cheniere Energy's stock is a proxy for the LNG export opportunity.

### Downstream (Refining, Chemicals, Retail)
Companies like Valero, Phillips 66, Marathon Petroleum. Revenue comes from converting crude oil into refined products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, plastics). Margins depend on the "crack spread" — the difference between the price of refined products and the cost of crude input.

**Crack spread formula**: (3 × gasoline price + 2 × diesel price - 5 × crude oil price) / 5 = "3-2-1 crack spread." When crack spreads are wide ($20–30/bbl), refiners are highly profitable. When narrow ($5–10/bbl), margins compress.

### Integrated Oil Companies (IOCs)
ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell, BP, TotalEnergies operate across all three segments. The integration provides a natural hedge: when crude prices are high, upstream profits rise but refining margins compress (crude input is more expensive). This stability makes IOCs attractive to income-oriented investors.

### Renewable Energy
NextEra Energy, Enphase, First Solar, Vestas, Ørsted. Revenue from selling electricity from solar, wind, and storage assets. Power purchase agreements (PPAs) provide long-term revenue visibility. Key metric: Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) — the all-in cost per MWh over the project lifetime.

**Solar LCOE parity**: Utility-scale solar LCOE fell from $359/MWh in 2010 to ~$35/MWh in 2024 — a 90% decline driven by scale, manufacturing improvements, and polysilicon cost reduction. Solar is now the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most markets.

**Offshore wind economics**: Higher capacity factor than onshore (wind blows more consistently offshore), but installation costs are 2–3x onshore. European offshore wind (Ørsted, Vattenfall) faces profitability headwinds from supply chain inflation and rising interest rates (high capital intensity makes wind projects rate-sensitive).

## Commodity Cycle Mechanics

### Oil Price Drivers
Oil is priced globally. The main factors:
1. **OPEC+ supply decisions**: Saudi Arabia and Russia collectively control ~40% of global supply. Output cuts increase price; quota breaks decrease price. OPEC+ quota compliance is monitored via tanker tracking data.
2. **US shale production**: The US is now the world's largest oil producer at ~13M bbl/day. Shale is more responsive to price than conventional oil (6–12 month drill-to-production cycle vs. years for offshore).
3. **Global demand growth**: Driven by economic growth in developing markets (India, Southeast Asia) and petrochemical demand (plastics, fertilizers). China represents ~16% of global oil demand.
4. **Geopolitical supply disruptions**: Russia-Ukraine war, Iran sanctions, Libya, Venezuela instability.
5. **US dollar strength**: Oil is priced in USD globally — a strong dollar makes oil more expensive in local currency, suppressing demand.

### Natural Gas Price Drivers
- **Seasonal**: Heating in winter (highest demand Q4/Q1) and cooling in summer (Q2/Q3 peak in hot climates)
- **LNG exports**: US LNG exports create a global price link — when European TTF or Asian JKM prices are high, US gas gets exported, tightening US supply and lifting Henry Hub prices
- **Associated gas**: US shale oil wells produce associated gas as a byproduct — high oil prices lead to more drilling, more associated gas, and lower US gas prices

### How to Interpret Oil Price Moves
- Brent crude rise >$10/bbl sustained: bullish for upstream E&P stocks; neutral/bearish for refiners
- WTI/Brent spread widening (WTI discount expanding): US infrastructure congestion; bullish for Midland/Permian producers
- Henry Hub spike >$4/MMBtu: bullish for US LNG exporters; bearish for industrial chemical feedstock users
- Crack spread compression: refiners underperform; check if crude costs rose or product prices fell

## Energy Transition Dynamics

### Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, August 2022)
The most significant US clean energy legislation in history — $369B in climate/clean energy spending over 10 years.

Key provisions:
- **Production Tax Credit (PTC)**: $26/MWh for solar and wind electricity for 10 years
- **Investment Tax Credit (ITC)**: 30% tax credit for solar, storage, and other clean energy projects
- **EV tax credits**: $7,500 consumer credit for new EVs (income cap applies), $4,000 for used EVs
- **Clean hydrogen**: $3/kg production tax credit for green hydrogen (electrolysis from renewables)
- **Advanced manufacturing**: Tax credits for US-manufactured solar panels, wind turbines, battery cells

**Company-level impact**: NextEra Energy, First Solar, Enphase, Plug Power, Albemarle (lithium) are direct beneficiaries. Traditional oil companies (ExxonMobil, Chevron) are investing in carbon capture, hydrogen, and biofuels to access IRA credits.

**Risk**: IRA provisions are under political risk — a change in administration could modify or repeal some provisions.

### EU Energy Policy
- **REPowerEU**: European plan to end Russian gas dependence by 2027; accelerated LNG terminal buildout, renewable permitting reform
- **EU Emissions Trading System (ETS)**: Carbon price (~€50-70/tonne CO2) makes coal and gas more expensive; drives fuel switching to renewables
- **Critical Raw Materials Act (2024)**: EU mandating domestic sourcing for 10% of critical minerals by 2030; affects lithium, cobalt, rare earths supply chains

## Critical Minerals

The energy transition is highly materials-intensive. Key minerals and their supply chains:

### Lithium (Li-Ion Batteries)
- Primary use: EV batteries (75% of demand)
- Supply: "Lithium triangle" — Argentina, Bolivia, Chile (55% of reserves), plus Australia (hard rock spodumene)
- Price: Highly volatile; rose from $6,000/tonne (2020) to $80,000/tonne (2022) then fell to $15,000/tonne (2024) on supply surge
- Key companies: Albemarle, SQM, Livent, Arcadium Lithium
- Risk: China controls ~65% of lithium processing/refining capacity

### Cobalt
- Primary use: Cathodes in NMC (nickel-manganese-cobalt) batteries
- Supply: Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) produces ~70% of global cobalt; major geopolitical risk
- Trend: Battery manufacturers (CATL, Tesla, LG Energy Solution) are moving toward LFP (lithium iron phosphate) cathodes that use no cobalt; reducing exposure
- Key companies: Glencore (largest cobalt miner), Freeport-McMoRan (cobalt byproduct)

### Rare Earth Elements (REEs)
- Primary use: Permanent magnets in EV motors and wind turbines (neodymium, dysprosium)
- Supply: China produces ~60% of rare earths and controls ~90% of processing
- Risk: China has historically restricted rare earth exports as a geopolitical tool (2010 Japan dispute)
- US response: DoD funding for rare earth processing capacity; Lynas (Australia), MP Materials (US)

### Nickel
- Primary use: High-energy-density battery cathodes (NMC); also stainless steel
- Supply: Indonesia dominates nickel supply (~50% of global production)
- Risk: Indonesian nickel processed largely by Chinese companies (Tsingshan) — supply is abundant but geopolitically concentrated

## Key Metrics for EDA

| Metric | Formula | What It Means |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| Reserve Replacement Ratio (RRR) | New reserves added / Production | >100% = company replacing what it's extracting |
| Finding & Development (F&D) Cost | Capex / New reserves added (in $/boe) | Lower = more efficient at finding oil |
| Lifting Cost | Operating cost / Barrels of oil equivalent produced | Cash cost to extract; must be < market price |
| Netback | Selling price - Royalties - Transportation - Operating costs | Revenue per boe after all costs |
| Crack Spread | Refined product price - Crude input cost | Refiner profitability |
| Reserve Life Index (RLI) | Proven reserves / Annual production | How many years of production at current rate |
| EBITDA / Boe | EBITDA / Barrels of oil equivalent produced | Profitability per unit of production |
| Capacity Factor | Actual energy output / Maximum possible output | Renewable asset efficiency (wind: ~35-45%, solar: ~20-25%) |
| LCOE | All-in cost per MWh over project lifetime | Renewable competitiveness vs. other sources |

### How to Interpret Revenue Movements for Energy Companies
- **Revenue spike with oil price increase**: Primarily price-driven, not operational; check volumes separately
- **Revenue growth despite flat prices**: Volume growth = new wells, acquisitions, or asset ramp-up
- **Revenue decline with stable prices**: Production decline (depletion), asset sale, or volume disruption
- **Crack spread collapse with stable crude**: Refining margin compression; seasonal or structural oversupply of refined products
- **LNG revenue growth without US gas price move**: Export volumes growing; check LNG terminal utilization rates

### EDA Playbook for Energy & Materials
1. Always separate price effect from volume effect in revenue analysis
2. Compute lifting cost as a % of realized price — companies near breakeven are high-risk
3. Map reserve replacement ratio — below 100% = reserves depleting faster than replenishment
4. For renewables: LCOE trend is more important than current margin (it shows competitive position)
5. Check capex trajectory — energy transition requires massive investment; high capex signals either growth or financial stress
6. For critical minerals: check end-market exposure (EV penetration rate is the key demand driver)
7. Identify commodity price sensitivity: a $10/bbl oil price change typically affects upstream EBITDA by 10–20%
